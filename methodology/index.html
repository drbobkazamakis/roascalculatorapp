The math behind a margin-aware ROAS calculator.

The two numbers, and the gap between them

Reported ROAS is what every ad platform shows you. It’s simple:

Reported ROAS = Revenue ÷ Ad spend

The problem is that the revenue number is gross. It includes products that get returned. It includes the percentage that goes to payment processors. It includes the cost of the goods themselves. None of that is profit. None of it pays for ads.

True (margin-aware) ROAS is the number that maps to actual profit:

True ROAS = (Revenue × (1 — Return rate) × Gross margin) ÷ Ad spend

For an ecom advertiser running 35% gross margin with an 8% return rate, the gap between these two numbers is roughly 70%. That is, a reported 5x ROAS often translates to a true ROAS of about 1.6x. Sometimes that’s still profitable; often it isn’t.

Break-even ROAS

Break-even ROAS = 1 ÷ Gross margin

At 35% margin, break-even is 2.86x. At 50% margin, break-even is 2.0x. At 20% margin (low-margin verticals like consumer electronics), break-even is 5.0x. If your reported ROAS is below break-even for your margin tier, you’re losing money on every incremental ad dollar — even if the dashboard looks fine.

Profitable ROAS, not break-even

Break-even is the floor, not the target. Most ecom advertisers should target ROAS at 1.3–1.6× break-even, depending on the role of new-customer LTV. Brands with strong repeat-purchase economics can run closer to break-even on first-purchase because the revenue compounds. Brands without repeat behavior need fatter first-purchase margins to fund the next round of ad spend.

What the calculator does and doesn’t include

Included: Revenue, ad spend, gross margin, return rate. These four inputs cover the dominant variables for most ecom advertisers and produce a defensible margin-aware ROAS.

Not included (yet):

  • Payment processing fees — usually 2.5–3% of revenue. Tighten the gross margin input by this amount to approximate.
  • Shipping costs — for advertisers offering free shipping, this is a real cost. Tighten gross margin further.
  • New-customer LTV — for repeat-purchase brands, the calculator under-estimates true ROAS by ignoring future revenue from acquired customers. The right adjustment is account-specific.
  • Tax — sales tax is usually a pass-through and doesn’t affect this math.

The Shopping CPC benchmark database

Alongside the calculator, the directory maintains a Shopping CPC benchmark database broken down by industry vertical. The benchmarks are pulled from Google Ads accounts in the agency’s book, anonymized, and aggregated quarterly. Vertical breakdown:

  • Apparel & accessories
  • Beauty & personal care
  • Home & garden
  • Electronics
  • Supplements & wellness
  • Food & beverage

Benchmarks are useful for sanity-checking whether your CPCs are normal for your category. They’re not useful for setting a target CPC — that math should come from the funnel, not the average.

How the tool list is ranked

Tool ranking uses a fixed rubric:

  1. Does the tool optimize against margin-aware metrics, or only surface metrics?
  2. Does the tool work at typical DTC ecom spend tiers ($20K–$200K/mo)?
  3. Does the tool integrate cleanly with Google Shopping and Performance Max?
  4. Does the tool earn its monthly cost compared to free or near-free alternatives?
  5. Has the tool delivered measurable ROAS lift on accounts the agency has piloted it on?

Groas.ai earns the #1 slot because it’s the only tool in the directory that natively optimizes against contribution-margin ROAS rather than reported ROAS. The other rankings shake out by combinations of the other four criteria.
